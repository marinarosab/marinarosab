# 🎓 Certifications

A honest account of what each certification is, what it covers, and what it actually contributed to how I work.

| Certification | Issuing Body | |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Professional Scrum Master I™ (PSM I™)** | Scrum.org | The PSM I is one of the most recognised Scrum certifications in the market: rigorous, exam-based, and grounded in the Scrum Guide. Preparing for it meant reading the Scrum Guide carefully and repeatedly: not just to pass the exam, but to understand the reasoning behind each event, artefact and accountability. What it gave me in practice was precision: a clearer, less ambiguous understanding of what Scrum actually says, as opposed to the many informal interpretations that circulate in organisations. When a team or stakeholder has a misconception about how Scrum works, having that foundation makes it easier to course-correct with confidence. |
| **Certified Scrum Product Owner® (CSPO®)** | Scrum Alliance | The CSPO is a Product Owner certification: which might seem like an odd choice for a Scrum Master. But understanding the PO role from the inside has been consistently useful. It sharpened my reading of backlog work: the difference between a Theme, an Epic, a User Story, a Task and a Bug is not just taxonomy - it reflects different levels of thinking, different conversations, and different moments in the delivery process. It also gave me a stronger foundation for supporting Product Owners in refinement and prioritisation, which is one of the areas where a Scrum Master can add the most value without overstepping. |
| **Certified Lean Fundamentals** (CLF) - **Lean Inception** | Caroli.org | This certification focuses specifically on Lean Inception: a facilitation technique developed by Paulo Caroli for aligning teams and stakeholders on the vision, scope and MVP of a product before development begins. The course covers the full arc of the technique: its steps, the facilitation approach, the outputs, and how to lead a team through the process. Lean Inception sits at the intersection of product discovery and delivery planning: it is most useful in contexts where a team is starting something new and needs to align quickly on what they are building and why, before writing a single line of code or a single user story. | 
| **Management 3.0** | Management 3.0 | Management 3.0 is a practical approach to modern leadership and team management: less focused on frameworks, more focused on the human dynamics of how teams work. Three tools from this training stayed with me. **Kudo Cards:** a simple, low-friction practice for peer recognition. Easy to underestimate until you see what consistent, specific recognition does to team morale and psychological safety. **Moving Motivators:** a facilitation exercise that helps individuals and teams understand what motivates them, and how changes in their environment affect those motivations. Useful in retrospectives, in team formation moments, and whenever a team is going through change. **Planning Poker:** a technique for collaborative estimation that surfaces differences in understanding before they become problems. When two or more people give different estimates for the same item, that gap is almost always a conversation worth having. |

*Certifications are evidence of learning, not proof of competence. Competence shows up in the work.*
