# Agile Glossary · EN / PT
A reference guide for agile practitioners working across English and Portuguese contexts. Each entry includes the term in both languages and a plain-language explanation in English.

Terms are organised alphabetically by the English term.

## A
**Acceptance Criteria** · *Critérios de Aceitação*
The conditions that a Product Backlog Item must meet to be considered complete. Acceptance criteria are specific to each story and define what "done" looks like at PBI level. They are distinct from the Definition of Done, which applies to all work.

**Agile** · *Ágil*
An umbrella term for a set of values, principles and practices for developing products and delivering work iteratively and collaboratively. Agile is not a methodology: it is a mindset. Scrum, Kanban, SAFe and XP are frameworks that implement agile principles in different ways.

## B
**Backlog** · *Backlog*
An ordered list of work items representing everything known about what a team needs to do. The Product Backlog belongs to the product; the Sprint Backlog belongs to the team for that sprint. Backlogs are living documents — they are continuously refined.

**Backlog Refinement** · *Refinamento do Backlog*
A recurring activity in which the Product Owner and the team review, clarify, estimate and order upcoming backlog items. Refinement ensures that items are ready before they enter a sprint. Also known as Backlog Grooming (an older term, less commonly used now).

**Board** · *Quadro*
A visual representation of work in progress, typically divided into columns (e.g. To Do, In Progress, Done). Used in both Scrum and Kanban to make work visible and identify bottlenecks.

**Burn-down Chart** · *Gráfico de Burn-down*
A chart that shows the amount of work remaining in a sprint or release over time. As work is completed, the line burns down toward zero. Used to track progress and spot early signs of risk.

## C
**Capacity** · *Capacidade*
The amount of work a team can realistically take on in a sprint, based on team size, availability, and historical velocity. Capacity is not the same as velocity — capacity is planned, velocity is measured.

**Ceremony** · *Cerimónia*
A structured event in Scrum: Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, and Sprint Retrospective. The term "ceremony" is sometimes criticised for implying ritual without purpose — the intent is for each event to have a clear goal and timebox.

**Cycle Time** · *Tempo de Ciclo*
The time elapsed from when work on an item begins to when it is complete. A flow metric used to understand how long the team takes to deliver once they start something. Shorter cycle times generally indicate healthier flow.

## D
**Daily Scrum** · *Scrum Diário / Daily*
A short daily event (15 minutes) for the development team to inspect progress toward the sprint goal and adapt the plan for the next 24 hours. It is not a status report for the Scrum Master or manager.

**Definition of Done (DoD)** · *Definição de Feito*
A shared, explicit agreement on the quality criteria that must be met for a work item (story, Feature, or increment) to be considered complete. The DoD ensures consistent quality standards across the team.

**Definition of Ready (DoR)** · *Definição de Pronto*
A shared agreement on the criteria that must be met before a backlog item can enter a sprint. The DoR protects the team from starting work that is unclear, incomplete, or blocked.

**Dependency** · *Dependência*
A relationship between two work items, teams, or systems where one cannot start or complete until the other has delivered something. Dependencies increase risk and should be identified and managed proactively.

## E
**Epic** · *Épico*
A large body of work that can be broken down into smaller pieces (Features or stories). Epics represent significant business objectives and typically span multiple sprints or PIs. They are too large to deliver as a single unit.

**Estimation** · *Estimativa*
The process of sizing backlog items to support planning. Common estimation techniques include Story Points (relative sizing), T-shirt sizes (S/M/L/XL), and hours. Estimates are approximations, not commitments.

## F
**Feature** · *Funcionalidade*
A piece of functionality that delivers value to a user or stakeholder. Features sit between Epics and Product Backlog Items in the hierarchy. A Feature is typically too large to deliver in a single sprint and is broken down into PBIs.

**Flow** · *Fluxo*
The smooth, continuous movement of work through a system. Good flow means work moves from start to finish without unnecessary waiting, blocking, or rework. Flow metrics (cycle time, lead time, throughput, CFD) are used to measure and improve it.

## I
**Impediment** · *Impedimento*
Anything that slows down or blocks the team from making progress. The Scrum Master is responsible for helping remove impediments — some can be resolved within the team, others require escalation.

**Increment** · *Incremento*
The sum of all completed Product Backlog Items at the end of a sprint. Each increment should be potentially releasable — meaning it meets the Definition of Done and adds value to the product.

**Iteration** · *Iteração*
A fixed-length period of development (synonymous with "sprint" in Scrum contexts). Working iteratively means delivering value in small, regular cycles rather than all at once.

## K
**Kanban** · *Kanban*
A method for managing and improving flow of work, based on visualising work, limiting work in progress (WIP), and measuring flow. Kanban does not prescribe iterations or roles — it can be used alongside Scrum or independently.

## L
**Lead Time** · *Tempo de Entrega*
The time elapsed from when a work item is requested (or enters the backlog) to when it is complete. Lead time includes waiting time, whereas cycle time starts only when work actively begins.

## P
**PI Planning** · *Planeamento de PI*
Program Increment Planning — a large-scale planning event used in SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework) where multiple teams align on a shared set of objectives for the next 8–12 weeks. Teams identify dependencies, set sprint goals, and commit to PI objectives.

**Product Backlog** · *Backlog do Produto*
An ordered list of everything known that might be needed in the product — features, improvements, fixes, technical work. Owned and prioritised by the Product Owner. It is never complete; it evolves as the product and understanding evolve.

**Product Backlog Item (PBI)** · *Item do Backlog do Produto*
A single item in the Product Backlog. PBIs can take different forms — User Stories, tasks, bugs, spikes. In Azure DevOps, "Product Backlog Item" is the specific work item type used in Scrum process templates.

**Product Owner (PO)** · *Dono do Produto*
The Scrum role responsible for maximising the value of the product by managing and prioritising the Product Backlog. The PO represents the needs of stakeholders and customers, and is the single point of authority on what the team builds and in what order.

## R
**Refinement** · *Refinamento*
See Backlog Refinement.

**Retrospective** · *Retrospectiva*
A ceremony held at the end of each sprint in which the team reflects on how they worked together and identifies improvements to their process. The retrospective is for the team — not a performance review, not a status meeting.

## S
**Scrum** · *Scrum*
An agile framework for developing and delivering products iteratively. Scrum defines three roles (Product Owner, Scrum Master, Developers), five events (Sprint, Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, Sprint Retrospective), and three artefacts (Product Backlog, Sprint Backlog, Increment).

**Scrum Master** · *Scrum Master*
The Scrum role responsible for helping the team understand and apply Scrum effectively, removing impediments, and facilitating events. The Scrum Master serves the team, the Product Owner, and the organisation — not a project manager or team lead.

**Sprint** · *Sprint*
A fixed-length event (usually 1–4 weeks) during which the team works to deliver a potentially releasable increment. Sprints provide a regular rhythm for delivery, inspection, and adaptation.

**Sprint Backlog** · *Backlog da Sprint*
The set of Product Backlog Items selected for a sprint, plus the plan for delivering them. Owned by the development team.

**Sprint Goal** · *Objetivo da Sprint*
A short statement describing what the team aims to achieve during the sprint. The sprint goal gives the team focus and flexibility — if something changes mid-sprint, the team can adapt their plan while still pursuing the goal.

**Sprint Review** · *Revisão da Sprint*
A ceremony at the end of the sprint in which the team presents the increment to stakeholders and collects feedback. It is a collaborative working session, not a formal presentation or demo.

**Stakeholder** · *Parte Interessada*
Anyone with an interest in or who is affected by the product — customers, users, business owners, regulators. In Scrum, stakeholders participate in the Sprint Review and provide input to the Product Owner.

**Story Points** · *Pontos de Story*
A unit used to estimate the relative size and complexity of backlog items. Story points are not hours — they measure effort, complexity, and uncertainty relative to other items. Teams define their own scale.

## T
**Team Agreement** · *Acordo de Equipa*
A set of working norms agreed by the team — how they communicate, make decisions, handle conflict, and support each other. Team agreements make implicit expectations explicit.

**Throughput** · *Throughput*
The number of work items completed per unit of time (e.g. 8 PBIs per sprint). A flow metric used alongside cycle time and lead time to understand team capacity and predict delivery.

**Tribe** · *Tribo*
A group of squads aligned around a common mission or product area, used in scaled agile contexts (e.g. Spotify model, SAFe). A tribe typically contains 3–8 squads.

## U
**User Story** · *História de Utilizador*
A short description of a feature or capability from the perspective of the person who will use it, typically written as: "As a [user], I want to [action] so that [benefit]." User stories are a tool for conversation — they are not requirements documents.

## V
**Velocity** · *Velocidade*
The amount of work a team completes in a sprint, measured in story points or number of PBIs. Velocity is used for planning and forecasting — it is an indicator, not a performance target.

## W
**WIP (Work in Progress)** · *Trabalho em Curso*
The number of work items actively being worked on at any given time. Limiting WIP is a core Kanban principle — too much work in progress increases context-switching, delays, and quality issues.
