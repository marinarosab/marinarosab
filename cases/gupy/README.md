# 🚀 Case · Gupy - An Agile Lens on SaaS Implementation

- **Type:** Professional experience 
- **Role:** Implementation Project Manager  
- **Period:** May 2021 – September 2022  
- **Context:** Gupy · São Paulo, Brazil  

## Context
Gupy is a Brazilian HR technology platform used by hundreds of companies to manage recruitment, selection and onboarding processes. As an Implementation Project Manager, I led the full journey from signed contract to go-live - managing scope, timelines, client relationships, platform configuration, training and adoption.

My title was Implementation Project Manager. But looking back at how I actually worked, the underlying patterns were agile ones - even if that vocabulary was not always in use.

This case is not a claim that I was running Scrum at Gupy. It is an honest reflection on what implementation work at that level of complexity actually requires, and how it maps to the delivery thinking I have since developed and formalised.

## What implementation work actually looked like

- ### Structured cadence with clear deliverables per cycle:
Every implementation was organised around milestones — fixed points in time with defined outcomes that needed to be reached before the next phase could begin. Each milestone had a meeting with the client where we reviewed what had been completed, aligned on what was coming next, and confirmed that both sides were ready to move forward.

In practice, this meant that every cycle had:
- A clear goal (what needed to be ready by the next session)
- A shared understanding of who was responsible for what — on our side and the client's
- A review moment to inspect progress and adapt the plan if needed

This is sprint thinking. The vocabulary was different, but the logic was the same: short cycles, defined deliverables, regular inspection, continuous adaptation.

### Defining what "ready" and "done" meant at each stage

One of the recurring challenges in implementation work is misaligned expectations: the client thinks something is done; the implementation team thinks it still needs configuration or testing. Or the client has not completed their side of the preparation, and the session has to be rescheduled.

Managing this meant being explicit about what needed to be true before each milestone meeting could happen — and what needed to be true for that milestone to be closed. This is, in essence, a Definition of Ready and a Definition of Done — applied not to user stories, but to implementation phases.

### Stakeholder alignment and follow-up cadence

Enterprise implementations involve many people: HR leads, IT contacts, procurement, managers who will be trained, sometimes senior leadership who need to approve decisions. Keeping all of these people aligned — with the right information, at the right moment — was a core part of the work.

I maintained a regular follow-up cadence with each stakeholder group: not just to chase approvals, but to make sure blockers were surfaced early, decisions were made before they became critical, and no one arrived at a milestone meeting without context.

This is flow management. The goal was to keep work moving without unnecessary waiting — which is exactly what a Scrum Master does when removing impediments and maintaining communication between the team and its stakeholders.

### Managing multiple implementations in parallel

At any given time, I was managing several enterprise implementations simultaneously — each at a different stage, with different clients, different scopes and different dynamics.

This required the same skills that multi-team coordination requires: knowing the state of each "team" (client project) at any moment, identifying which ones needed attention, managing dependencies between my own availability and their timelines, and making sure nothing fell through the gaps while context-switching between very different situations.

---

## What this reframe reveals

The implementation role at Gupy was not an agile role in name. But it required iterative thinking, structured cadences, stakeholder management, expectation alignment, and the ability to manage multiple complex workstreams in parallel — all while keeping the end goal (a successful go-live and a client who knows how to use the platform) clearly in sight.

The difference between what I did then and what I do now as a Scrum Master is not a difference in mindset — it is a difference in framework, vocabulary and context. The underlying thinking was already there.

That is not a coincidence. It is what good delivery work looks like, regardless of what you call it.
